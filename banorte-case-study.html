Voice and text-based conversational AI for digital banking services

Role

Conversational UX Designer

Timeline

Team

Tools

Project Overview

The Challenge: Banorte, one of Mexico's largest banks, wanted to modernize customer service by implementing an AI-powered virtual assistant that could handle routine banking inquiries and transactions through both voice and text channels. The assistant needed to understand Mexican Spanish dialects, banking terminology, and provide a friendly yet professional experience that would reduce call center volume while maintaining customer satisfaction.

My Role: As Conversational UX Designer, I designed the end-to-end conversational experiences for Maya, Banorte's virtual assistant. I created dialogue flows, defined personality guidelines, wrote conversational scripts using grammar and SSML standards, and established error handling patterns. I worked in agile sprints to deliver 12 banking use cases, from balance inquiries to complex loan applications.

Key Responsibilities:

  • Designed conversational flows for 12 banking use cases including account inquiries, transfers, card services, and loan information
  • Created Maya's personality, tone of voice, and conversational guidelines to ensure consistent brand experience
  • Wrote SSML (Speech Synthesis Markup Language) scripts to control voice prosody, pausing, and emphasis for natural-sounding responses
  • Designed grammar patterns to handle Mexican Spanish variations, banking jargon, and common user input errors
  • Established fallback and error recovery patterns that gracefully handled misunderstandings without frustrating users
  • Conducted conversation testing with 50+ users to identify friction points and optimize dialogue flows
  • Collaborated with AI engineers to train natural language understanding models and improve intent recognition
  • Worked across 9 agile sprints to iteratively deliver and refine banking use cases

Design Process & Methodology

1

Discovery & Research

Analyzed call center transcripts from 200+ customer service interactions to identify common banking queries, pain points, and conversation patterns. Conducted interviews with bank customers about their expectations for virtual assistants. Studied competitor chatbots and voice assistants to understand industry standards and identify opportunities for differentiation.

2

Personality Development

Established Maya's conversational personality as friendly, professional, and helpful‚Äîmirroring the experience of speaking with an expert bank representative. Created detailed tone of voice guidelines, sample phrases, and do's/don'ts to ensure consistency across all interactions. Defined how Maya would handle different customer emotions from frustrated to confused.

3

Dialogue Design

Created detailed conversation flows for each banking use case using dialogue mapping tools. Designed branching paths for different user intents, error states, and edge cases. Wrote conversational scripts with natural language variations to avoid robotic repetition. Implemented progressive disclosure to gather information step-by-step without overwhelming users.

4

Voice Optimization

Used SSML to fine-tune voice output for natural prosody, appropriate pausing, and emphasis on important information like account balances and transaction amounts. Tested different voice speeds and tones to find the optimal balance between clarity and naturalness. Created pronunciation guides for banking terms, account numbers, and Mexican Spanish variations.

User Pain Points

  • Long wait times calling customer service, especially for simple balance inquiries that could be self-service
  • Frustration with IVR (Interactive Voice Response) systems that required navigating complex menu trees
  • Difficulty remembering account numbers and other details needed for phone banking authentication
  • Confusion about banking terminology and reluctance to ask human agents "basic" questions
  • Skepticism about AI assistants based on poor experiences with robotic chatbots that couldn't understand them
  • Concern about security when providing sensitive banking information to automated systems

Design Opportunities

  • Design conversational shortcuts for common tasks like balance checks and recent transactions
  • Create natural language understanding that accepts multiple ways of asking the same question
  • Build contextual authentication that doesn't require users to remember specific account numbers
  • Provide patient, judgment-free explanations of banking concepts and processes
  • Design personality that feels human-like while being transparent about being a virtual assistant
  • Implement clear security indicators and explanations to build trust in the AI system

Key Design Decisions

Building Maya required careful attention to conversational nuances and cultural context for Mexican banking customers:

1. Bilingual Capability with Regional Variations: Designed Maya to handle both formal and informal Mexican Spanish, including regional expressions and slang. Created grammar patterns that recognized different ways customers might express the same intent‚Äîfor example, "saldo" vs "cu√°nto tengo" vs "mi dinero" all meaning balance inquiry. This flexibility made conversations feel natural rather than forcing users to learn specific commands.

2. Progressive Confidence Building: Started users with simple, low-risk interactions (balance checks, transaction history) before introducing complex tasks (transfers, loan applications). This allowed customers to build confidence in Maya's capabilities gradually. Designed confirmations and summaries at each step to maintain user trust, especially for financial transactions.

3. Graceful Error Recovery: Rather than generic "I didn't understand" messages, designed context-aware error handling that offered helpful suggestions. For example, if Maya didn't understand an account type, she would list available options rather than just asking users to repeat themselves. Implemented three-strike escalation to human agents when conversations weren't progressing to prevent user frustration.

4. Voice-Optimized Responses: Designed different response patterns for voice vs text channels. Voice responses were more concise and used natural pausing, while text responses could include additional details and formatting. Used SSML to add appropriate emotional tone‚Äîenthusiasm when confirming successful transfers, empathy when handling problems, clarity when stating important numbers.

5. Banking Security Without Friction: Designed authentication flows that balanced security requirements with conversational naturalness. Rather than asking for lengthy account numbers upfront, Maya used contextual verification (recent transactions, registered phone number) that felt like natural conversation while maintaining security standards required by banking regulations.

6. Educational Moments: Embedded brief educational explanations when users encountered unfamiliar banking terms or processes. Rather than assuming knowledge, Maya offered optional "Would you like me to explain what this means?" prompts that empowered users without being condescending to those who already understood.

12 Banking Use Cases Delivered

Across 9 agile sprints, we designed and implemented 12 conversational banking use cases:

  • Balance Inquiry: Quick account balance checks across checking, savings, and credit card accounts
  • Transaction History: Recent transactions with filtering by date, amount, and merchant
  • Fund Transfers: Between-account transfers and third-party transfers with confirmation flows
  • Card Services: Card activation, blocking, PIN changes, and reporting lost/stolen cards
  • Loan Information: Current loan balances, payment schedules, and payoff amounts
  • Bill Payment: Utility bill payments and registered biller management
  • Branch Locator: Finding nearest branches and ATMs with operating hours
  • Product Information: Details about savings accounts, credit cards, and loan products
  • Statement Requests: Requesting digital account statements for specific periods
  • Direct Deposit Setup: Guided setup for payroll direct deposit enrollment
  • Customer Profile Updates: Changing contact information, email, and notification preferences
  • General Banking FAQs: Answers to common questions about fees, policies, and procedures

Key Learnings

Cultural Context in Conversation Design: Designing for Mexican Spanish required more than translation‚Äîit required understanding cultural communication norms. We learned that Mexican customers preferred warmer, more personal interactions than typical American or European chatbots. Maya's personality evolved to include appropriate greetings, culturally relevant expressions, and the right balance of formal/informal address based on context.

Voice vs Text Require Different Approaches: Early in the project, we tried using identical responses for both channels. User testing revealed this didn't work‚Äîvoice needed shorter, more conversational responses while text could include more detail and formatting. We learned to design channel-specific experiences while maintaining consistent personality and capabilities across both.

User Trust Must Be Earned Gradually: Customers were initially skeptical about trusting an AI with banking tasks. We learned that starting with low-stakes interactions (balance checks) and demonstrating reliability built confidence for more complex tasks (transfers, payments). Each successful interaction increased willingness to try more sophisticated features.

Error Handling Makes or Breaks Experience: The difference between frustrating and helpful AI was how gracefully it handled misunderstandings. Generic error messages ("I didn't understand") caused users to abandon conversations. Context-aware error recovery with helpful suggestions ("Did you mean checking or savings account?") kept conversations productive and maintained user confidence.

Banking Jargon Requires Translation: Even native Spanish speakers struggled with banking terminology. We learned to provide plain-language explanations without being condescating, offering optional "would you like to know more?" prompts that empowered users without assuming ignorance.

SSML Detail Matters for Voice Quality: Small adjustments in voice timing, emphasis, and tone significantly impacted perceived naturalness. We learned that reading numbers (especially currency amounts) required careful SSML markup‚Äîappropriate pauses, emphasis on important digits, and clear enunciation of decimals made voice interactions feel professional and trustworthy.

Agile Methodology Suited Conversational Design: Two-week sprints allowed us to rapidly test dialogue flows with real users and iterate. We learned to treat each use case as a minimum viable conversation, launching basic functionality quickly and refining based on usage data and feedback rather than trying to perfect everything upfront.

Collaboration with AI Engineers Was Essential: As conversation designers, we needed to understand NLP capabilities and limitations. Close collaboration with AI engineers helped us design realistic dialogue flows that the technology could support while pushing capabilities forward through training data improvements based on user conversations.
